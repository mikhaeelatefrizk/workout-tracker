⚕ Before you start — health first

  • Get a quick doctor check given your starting numbers (BMI 36.8, visceral fat 14): resting blood pressure, fasting glucose/HbA1c, a lipid panel and liver enzymes.
  • Don't load heavy until your blood pressure is confirmed not high. On every hard rep, exhale through the effort — never hold your breath and strain. Dizzy, seeing stars, head or chest pressure → rack it and stop.
  • If a joint (shoulder, elbow, wrist) hurts during a movement — not just muscle burn — stop that exercise and go lighter. Loud snoring or daytime sleepiness? Ask your doctor about a sleep-apnea screen.

◆ The goal — recomposition

  • You already carry a strong muscle base (≈39 kg of muscle) hidden under the fat. The plan is to lose fat — especially the visceral fat — while keeping (and likely adding a little) muscle, so you end up looking leaner AND more muscular.
  • Honest timeline: the first 12 weeks is mostly fat loss and sharper definition — real muscle gain is modest and not guaranteed. Abs are a 6–12-month job, not a 12-week one. The first 2–3 weeks of scale drop is partly water.
  • The scale will undersell you (muscle up, fat down). Judge progress by photos, a waist measurement, and the same InBody scan every 4–8 weeks — not bodyweight alone.

🍽 Eat — this is what actually drives it

  • Start at ~2,400 kcal/day (≈2,350–2,450). Hold it for 2–3 weeks and watch your weekly-average weight: aim for ~0.6–0.8 kg/week down. Dropping faster than ~0.9 kg/week → eat a bit more (you'll keep more muscle).
  • Protein ~160 g/day — set off your lean mass, not the scale. This is the single most important food rule for keeping muscle while dieting. (Assumes healthy kidneys — worth confirming in that check-up.)
  • Fill the rest with ~60–70 g fat and the remainder carbs, mostly around training. Keep fiber and water high — they kill hunger at this calorie level.

🚶 Move — legs are out, so this matters MORE

  • 8,000–10,000 steps every day, training and rest days. At your bodyweight this quietly burns the most fat of anything you'll do — it replaces the calories a leg day would have burned.
  • 3× per week, 30–40 min easy cardio at a conversational pace — incline-treadmill walk, bike, elliptical, or rower (not running yet, at your weight). After lifting or on off days. This is also what lowers your visceral fat the most.
  • Steps + protein, hit daily, decide your results more than any exercise choice.

🏋 Lift — 3 days, A → B → C

  • Each muscle is trained 2–3× a week. Back gets the most — it's your biggest payoff and builds the V-taper that makes your waist look smaller.
  • Double progression: beat last session by a rep; hit the top of the range on every set and the Coach adds weight and resets you. Keep 1–2 reps in reserve on the compounds.
  • Deload one light week when your logged lifts stall ~2 weeks across several exercises, or sleep/joints/appetite tank — or just every 6–8 weeks. In a deficit, deload a little early rather than grind.
  • Later, for your health, it's worth adding some leg work back once you're settled in — lower-body muscle is one of the best tools against the exact visceral-fat / blood-sugar risk you're targeting. Your call, no rush.
  • Lead with the big lift. Do the first (compound) exercise while you're freshest — log its weight × reps first.
  • Leave 1–2 reps in the tank on compounds. Only the last set of cable/machine isolations goes to failure. Form > weight, always.
  • Breathe — never strain silently. Exhale as you push through the hardest part of every rep. Dizzy, seeing stars, or head/chest pressure → rack it and stop.
  • Progression: add a rep each session; hit the top of the range on all sets → the Coach adds weight and you reset to the bottom → climb again.
  • Volume drives growth: aim 10–20 hard sets per muscle per week — 📊 Stats counts them for you. More isn't always better past ~20.
  • Rest ~2–3 min on the heavy compounds, ~60–120 sec on isolations. Use the timer below.
  • Log every set. If the numbers aren't climbing over a few weeks, fix sleep, food, or effort — that's the signal, not more exercises.
